    Smart Communicating material for Structural Health Monitoring - State of the art

    Context

    During my 4th year at INSA, I participated in a research project about Smart communicating material for Structural Health Monitoring. The first part of the project consisted in realizing a state of the art of wireless technologies that could be employed to perform Building Monitoring. On one hand, we would have sensors able to perform aging, humidity and other sorts of measurements inside the structure, on the other hand we needed to be able to access this data. Therefore, The goal was to find a wireless very low power technology able to transmit through reinforced concrete walls.

    My role

    This state of the art was an introduction to the world of research. There was several steps to writing this paper. Firstly, I had to understand the subject of the research on smart communicating material for structural health monitoring. To select the technologies we would describe in our state of the art, we needed to grasp the constraints than came with working in the building monitoring environment. Then, one of our first task was to list all of the possible technologies that would respect the identified constraints and our hypothesis on other possible constraints. We were looking for wireless, low power technologies. Once it was done, I worked specifically on LoRa, UWB and RFID. I searched for information on how each of these technologies worked, how they were implemented but first and foremost about their data rate, their power consumption and their range. These were the key element to know wether the technology was relevant to our application or not.

    State of the art

    Smart Communicating material for Structural Health Monitoring - Experimental study

    Context

    There was a second dimension to this research project. After having made an inventory of most of the available technologies for our application and having compared them, we had to check if the theoretical data was correct. In this experimental part, we picked 3 technologies, LoRa, UWB and BLE. We studied their power consumption and range, both in free space and when the emitting device was located inside a reinforced concrete box. Due to material issues, the final paper is only about BLE and LoRa and mostly focused on the latter.

    My role

    The first phase of this side of the project was selecting which technologies would be the most relevant to put to the test. Once we had decided on LoRa, BLE and UWB, we started writing the protocols on how we would proceed to the range and power consumption measurements. I focused on LoRa, so in order to measure the power consumption I had to learn how to program a microcontroller. I implemented a routine of sleeping/waking-up/emitting for the RN2483 LoRa module so we could measure the power consumption at different times and various data rate. To measure the range, we had to make a reinforced concrete box prototype to test the propagation through reinforced concrete in a non-destructive manner. We only had time to look into the BLE board, but there was some technical issues with it that prevented us from emitting and therefore from measuring the power consumption and the range properly. So my main activities were to measure the power consumption of the LoRa technologies in different states and at different data rate and to measure its range in a free-space environment and when forced to go through reinforced concrete.

    Hackhaton : Interactive visit of the aquarium of Banyuls

    Context

    This hackhaton was organized jointly with the oceanologic observatory of Banuyls-sur-Mer. We worked for two days in teams from the ISS PTP to propose an innovative project. We were free to choose what we wanted to work on but it needed to be useful to either the National Nature Reserve of La Massane or the aquarium of Banuyls-sur-Mer or researchers from the observatory. We started by thinking of a few ideas, and we finally settled on making a prototype for an interactive visit of the Aquarium.

    My role

    We thought of an interactive visit of the aquarium through an application that would connect to different game pannels disseminated around the aquarium. The visitor would download the app, connect to the different game pannels. Every questions answered or game played on the app would result in the activation of light or sound signals on the game panel. Finally, once all the questions were answered the visitor would receive a code to enter on the board pannel to check if the answers were correct and set off the board's response accordingly. There was a lot to do to obtain a working prototype in 48 hours. Each of us worked on different aspect. I participated in two different tasks in terms of microcontroller programming and application development. I worked on a QR code solution to pair the application with the right game panel. Due to a lack of time, we did not have time to implement this part. Then, I also worked on the game pannel. I made a LEDs circuit and programmed an Arduino to provide constant blinking light signals attracting the eyes of the visitors to the board.

    PCB Conception of an Arduino shield with a gas sensor

    Context

    During the courses of Microcontroller and Open-Source Hardware and KiCAD of the ISS training, we conceived the Printed Circuit Board of an Arduino shield. With the software KiCAD, we designed this PCB. Previously, we had worked on the kind of circuit we needed to implement two functionnalities: gas detection and LoRa communication. Therefore, our shield integrate a gas sensor able to detect the presence of a gas and a LoRa chip to enable long range low power communication of the gas detection results.

    My role

    In teams of two people, we performed the several steps needed to realize the Arduino shield's PCB on the software. The first thing we did was discussing the kind of electronic components needed to perform the tasks presented earlier, and figuring out how they were wired together. This represent the schematics part of the realization. Once the circuit was represented, we had to associate or create prints if they did not already exist for each component to be able to perform the routing operation and visualize our PCB. The next step was to perform the routing, which means choosing the optimal path for each printed track between two components needing to be wired. Personally, my focus was on producing the schematics and creating the prints, then I assisted the person working with me in doing the routing. We had to jointly take the decision to incorporate our shield and our lora chip onto a larger board. We did not find any other way to keep the routing on one side only and thus to respect the requirement of the printing machine at INSA. The final step would have been to proceed to the actual printing of the PCB which we did not have time to do due to time constraints.

    Conception of a gas sensor using nanoparticles

    Context

    Following the conception of the PCB, we had the opportunity to participate more in depth in the making of the gas sensor using nanoparticles. By groups of nine students from the ISS training, we spent about the equivalent of a week at the Center for Micro-Nano Electronics(AIME). This was the opportunity for us to work in white rooms and with equipments and materials such as a Scanning Electron Microscope or chemical substances. It was an interesting introduction to the micro-nano electronics world and its specificity.

    My role

    The first part of the work was to proceed to chemical reactions to obtain the tungsten trioxide nanoparticles. We followed a protocol that enabled us to get a good amount of nanoparticles of the right size. Once we had the nanoparticle, we started working on the microelectronics part. To propery get the different layers of aluminium and polysilicium, we needed to proceed to optical treatments (à modifier) . With different masks, we get the different layers of materials in the right places. Most of this treatment was already done, we only had the "à compléter" layer left to do. Then, we waited while the board was heated. We only witnessed the next few steps. The cutting and wiring process is very meticulous and requires technical skills that comes with a specific training. However, we did do the dropping of nanoparticules on the sensor while polarising it to have a good spreading of the particles on the sensitive layer. Finally, we observed the nanoparticles dispersion on our sensor at the SEM. We got to see how a microscope this perfectionated works. During the last session, we performed the characterization of the sensor in terms of resistance in ambiant air and under gas environment. You can find this characterization in the datasheet below.

    Analysis and data processing: the evolution of female athletes in the Olympics between 1896 and 2016

    Context

    With the development of the Internet Of Things, and the multiplication of sensors gathering lots of data, it is necessary to know how to process and alayse this data. During the Analysis and data processing course we had to choose a dataset to work on. We needed to set a problematic and then process the data and present it in a way which enabled us to extract meaningful information. Then we used the information extracted from the graphs to demonstrate a fact related to the problematic. My teammate and I chose a dataset about athletes in the Olympic Games. We focused on the evolution of the presence of female athletes in the Olympic Games from the first modern Olympics to 2016.

    My role

    There was a few guidelines for the choice of the dataset. It needed to be big enough so as to have enough data to extract something relevant. It needed to be helpful to answer a certain problematic. The minimum length of the dataset was of a thousand observations. We chose one of 271k rows of observations for 15 different categories. Our dataset was showing the athletes and their results for the Olympic Games from 1896 to 2016. This dataset contained a lot of information regarding the Olympics and we had to focus on one single problematic. Each of us proposed a number of topics to explore. The perks of having the gender of each athlete were that we were able to work on the evolution of women's representation in the Olympics compared to the men's representation, in terms of number, body characteristics but also depending on the sports. Since we had a dataset this large, the data processing dimension of the project was very important to only gather the data we needed to make a point. I started by learning how to code using R which is a language I had never used before. This first step of processing the data in the most efficient way possible was crucial to the project: applying functions and treatment to our dataframes, selecting some information and getting it ready to be used in a graph. Once I had acquired the basics of R we had to settle on five questions to demonstrate the evolution of women athletes in the Olympics. Our first point was to show the evolution of male and female constestants in number. Our second theme was to study the impact of women rights on the results of female athletes which is something we did by correlating our dataset with another dataset showing a human development index dedicated to women. Then, our third axis was to study the female-to-male ratio for each sport. Finally, our fourth and fifth points were focused on the evolution of body characteristics and the age of last participation in the Olympics. I did a first selection and processing of the data for each axis and my teammate would reuse my code to optimize our treatment, make it more efficient and precise. Then we discussed which graph would be the most efficient to show each point clearly and we presented our data in the elected graphs. Finally, I commented the first three graphs to really exploit the information they were showing regarding our initial problematic.
